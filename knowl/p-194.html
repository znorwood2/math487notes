Back to our example with Situations A and B. In Situation A we have \(\cov(X,Y) = 0\) since \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent therefore uncorrelated. In Situation B, we have:
\begin{equation*} \cov(X,Y) = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y) = 0 - \frac14 = -\frac14. \end{equation*}
So \(X\) and \(Y\) are negatively correlated, meaning when \(X\) decreases then \(Y\) increases, and vice versa. More specifically,
\begin{equation*} \rho(X,Y) = \frac{-1/4}{\sqrt{1/4 \cdot 1/4}} = -1, \end{equation*}
which (as we will show) is as negative as possible. So \(X\) and \(Y\) in this example are as negatively correlated as possible.
