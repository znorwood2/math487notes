Solution 1.3.2.1.

There are \(365^{28}\) possible outcomes, each of which is equally likely. This is a good example where “counting the complement” works well. That is, instead of counting the event we are asked to count, we will count its complement, the event that no two students share a birthday.
The number of ways of assigning 28 distinct birthdays to 28 students is \(\binom{365}{28} 28!\text{,}\) the number of ways of choosing which 28 birthdays will be represented times the number of ways of assigning them to everyone in order. It may help to record another way of expressing this quantity:
\begin{equation*} \binom{365}{28} 28! = 365 \cdot 364 \cdot \cdots \cdot 338. \end{equation*}
Now we can simplify the probability that no two people share a birthday:
\begin{equation*} P(\text{no birthday shared}) = \frac{\binom{365}{28} 28!}{365^{28}} = \frac{365!}{337!} \cdot \frac{1}{365^{28}}. \end{equation*}
A computer reports that this quantity is \(\approx 0.3455\text{.}\) So the probability that at least two people share a birthday is about \(65\%\text{.}\)
