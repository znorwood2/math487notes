    Section 1.2 Conditional probability and independence

    Subsection 1.2.1 Conditional Probability

    Definition 1.2.1.

    If \(P(B) \gt 0\) then the conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\), written \(P(A | B)\text{,}\) is the quantity
    \begin{equation*} P(A | B) = \frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)} . \end{equation*}
    Note that the rearranged form \(P(A\cap B) = P(A | B) P(B)\) of the equation can be useful.

    Example 1.2.2.

    In September 40% of days are warm and the rest are cool. On warm days there is a 60% chance of rain; on cool days there is a 30% chance.
    1. What is the probability that it will rain on a September day?
    2. What is the probability that it is warm, given that it is raining?
    Solution.

    Theorem 1.2.3. The Law of Total Probability.

    Suppose that events \(B_1,\dots,B_n\) partition \(\Omega\text{.}\) Then for any event \(A\) we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A | B_i) P(B_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A\cap B_i) . \end{equation*}

    Subsection 1.2.2 Independence

    Definition 1.2.4.

    Events \(A\) and \(B\) in a probability space are independent if \(P(A\cap B) = P(A)P(B)\text{.}\)
    More generally, events \(A_i\text{,}\) \(i\in I\text{,}\) are mutually independent if for any finite subset \(J \subseteq I\) we have
    \begin{equation*} P\left( \bigcap_{i\in J} A_i \right) = \prod_{i\in J} P(A_i). \end{equation*}
    The \(A_i\) are said to be pairwise independent if for any distinct indices \(i,j\in I\) the two events \(A_i\) and \(A_j\) are independent.

    Remark 1.2.5.

    • Notice that \(P(A\cap B) = P(A | B) P(B) = P(B | A) P(A)\text{.}\) So, as long as \(P(A) \gt 0\) and \(P(B) \gt 0\) the independence of \(A\) and \(B\) is equivalent to either \(P(A | B) = P(A)\) or \(P(B | A) = P(B)\text{;}\) that is, information about either event doesn’t change the probability of the other.
    • Independence is not the same as disjointness; independent events \(A\) and \(B\) may or may not satisfy \(A \cap B = \emptyset\text{.}\)

    Example 1.2.6.

    Roll two fair 6-sided dice. Let \(A\) denote the event that the first roll is a 3, \(B\) denote the event that the second roll is a 4, and \(C\) denote the event that the sum of the two rolls equals 7.
    The events \(A\) and \(B\) are certainly independent: of the 36 possible outcomes, 6 are in \(A\) and 6 are in \(B\text{;}\) and exactly 1 is common to \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\)
    Show that the three events \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) are pairwise but not mutually independent.
    Solution.
    That \(A\) and \(B\) are independent is explained in the statement of the example. For \(A\) and \(C\text{,}\) first notice that \(P(A) = P(B) = P(C) = \tfrac16\text{.}\) Then notice that \(A \cap C = A \cap B\) and so we have
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap C) = P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) = P(A) P(C). \end{equation*}
    A similar argument shows that \(B\) and \(C\) are independent.
    But \(A \cap B \cap C = A \cap B\text{,}\) so the three events are not mutually independent:
    \begin{equation*} P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A \cap B) = \tfrac{1}{36} \ne (\tfrac16)^3. \end{equation*}

    Example 1.2.7.

    Draw one card from a standard 52-card deck. Show that suit is independent of rank; for example, show that drawing a jack is independent of drawing a heart.
    Solution.
    The probability of drawing a jack is \(\tfrac{4}{52} = \tfrac{1}{13}\text{,}\) and the probability of drawing a heart is \(\tfrac{13}{52} = \tfrac14\text{.}\) The probability of drawing the Jack of Hearts is \(\tfrac{1}{52} = \tfrac{1}{13} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4}\text{.}\)
