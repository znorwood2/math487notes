    Section 3.1 Definitions and examples

    If \(X\) and \(Y\) are random variables on the same probability space, then their cdfs \(F_X\) and \(F_Y\) tell us about how they are distributed, individually. But with only this information, we don‚Äôt know how they act relative to each other.

    Example 3.1.1.

    Situation A. Flip two coins and define
    \begin{equation*} X = \begin {cases} 1 \amp \text{1st is heads} \\ 0 \amp \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \qquad Y = \begin {cases} 1 \amp \text{2nd is heads} \\ 0 \amp \text{otherwise} \end {cases} . \end{equation*}
    Then \(X\) and \(Y\) are identically distributed; they both follow a \(\Bernoulli(1/2)\) distribution and therefore have the same pmf and cdf.
    Situation B. Now flip only one coin and consider \(X = 1_H\) and \(Y = 1_T\text{.}\) (That is, \(X\) is the indicator rv of the event that the result is a heads, and \(Y\) indicates whether the result is a tails.) Then \(X\) and \(Y\) are identically distributed \(\Bernoulli(1/2)\) random variables, just as in Situation A.
    But clearly these two situations are different! How do we capture that? It might occur to us to record joint probabilies in a 2D array:
    \begin{equation*} A: \begin {array} {c | cc} X / Y \amp 0 \amp 1 \\\hline 0 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/4 \\ 1 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/4 \end {array} \qquad B: \begin {array} {c | cc} X / Y \amp 0 \amp 1 \\\hline 0 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \\ 1 \amp 1/2 \amp 0 \end {array} \end{equation*}

    Definition 3.1.2.

    The joint mass function of discrete random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} f_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X = x \text{ and } Y = y). \end{equation*}

    Remark 3.1.3.

    1. Discrete random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent if and only if their joint mass function is the pointwise product of their mass functions:
      \begin{equation*} f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y) \quad \forall x,y \end{equation*}
      (This holds in Situation A of the example but not in Situation B.)
    2. The definition of joint mass function can be extended to more than two random variables. We probably won‚Äôt worry about that much, though.
    3. \(f_X(x) = \sum_y f(x,y)\) and \(f_Y(y) = \sum_x f(x,y)\text{.}\) Follows from the Law of Total Probability. Thus, we get the so-called marginal mass functions by taking sums down columns and rows in the joint mass function tables:
      \begin{equation*} A: \begin {array} {c | cc | c} X / Y \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp f_X \\\hline 0 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/2 \\ 1 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/4 \amp 1/2 \\\hline f_Y \amp 1/2 \amp 1/2 \amp \end {array} \qquad B: \begin {array} {c | cc | c} X / Y \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp f_X \\\hline 0 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \amp 1/2 \\ 1 \amp 1/2 \amp 0 \amp 1/2 \\\hline f_Y \amp 1/2 \amp 1/2 \amp \end {array} \end{equation*}
    In Situation A, \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated. In Situation B, they are correlated. By how much?

    Definition 3.1.4.

    The covariance of random variables \(X\) and \(Y\) is defined by
    \begin{equation*} \cov(X,Y) = E((X - EX)(Y - EY)). \end{equation*}
    The correlation coefficient of \(X\) and \(Y\) is
    \begin{equation*} \rho(X,Y) = \frac{ \cov(X,Y)}{\sqrt{\var(X)}\sqrt{\var(Y)}}, \end{equation*}
    provided \(\var(X)\) and \(\var(Y)\) are both nonzero.

    Remark 3.1.5.

    Some properties:
    1. \(\displaystyle \cov(X,X) = \var(X)\)
    2. Recall that \(\var(X) = E(X\cdot X) - E(X)E(X)\text{.}\) In a moment we will prove that \(\cov(X,Y) = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y).\)
    3. \(\cov(X,Y) = 0\) iff \(X\) and \(Y\) are uncorrelated. This follows immediately from the previous item.
    4. \(\abs{\rho(X,Y)} \le 1\text{.}\) (This will also be proved soon.)

    Lemma 3.1.6.

    For all random variables \(X\) and \(Y\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} \cov(X,Y) = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y). \end{equation*}

    Proof.

    Essentially the same proof we gave for the case \(X = Y\text{.}\) Just expand and use linearity of expectation:
    \begin{align*} \cov(X,Y) \amp = E(XY - YE(X) - XE(Y) + E(X)E(Y)) \\ \amp = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y) - E(X)E(Y) + E(X)E(Y) \\ \amp = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y) \end{align*}

    Example 3.1.7.

    Back to our example with Situations A and B. In Situation A we have \(\cov(X,Y) = 0\) since \(X\) and \(Y\) are independent therefore uncorrelated. In Situation B, we have:
    \begin{equation*} \cov(X,Y) = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y) = 0 - \frac14 = -\frac14. \end{equation*}
    So \(X\) and \(Y\) are negatively correlated, meaning when \(X\) decreases then \(Y\) increases, and vice versa. More specifically,
    \begin{equation*} \rho(X,Y) = \frac{-1/4}{\sqrt{1/4 \cdot 1/4}} = -1, \end{equation*}
    which (as we will show) is as negative as possible. So \(X\) and \(Y\) in this example are as negatively correlated as possible.

    Example 3.1.8.

    Suppose that \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) are indicator random variables for events \(A\) and \(B\text{.}\) We have the following:
    \begin{equation*} E(1_A) = P(A), \quad E(1_B) = P(B), \quad E(1_A 1_B) = P(A \cap B). \end{equation*}
    So the covariance of \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \cov(1_A,1_B) = P(A \cap B) - P(A) P(B) = P(B)( P(A \mid B) - P(A)). \end{equation*}
    This means that \(1_A\) and \(1_B\) are ...
    \begin{align*} \text{positively correlated} \amp \text{ iff } P(A \mid B) \gt P(A) ;\\ \text{negatively correlated} \amp \text{ iff } P(A \mid B) \lt P(A) ;\text { and} \\ \text{uncorrelated} \amp \text{ iff } P(A \mid B) = P(A). \end{align*}

    Flip a fair coin three times. Let \(X\) count the number of heads in the first 2 flips. Let \(Y\) count the number of heads in the last 2 flips. Compute \(\cov(X,Y)\text{.}\)
