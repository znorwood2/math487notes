    Section 1.1 The Axioms of Probability

    Intuition can mislead us, particularly in probability, so we make an effort to be careful from the start.
    But let‚Äôs motivate the definition and consider an example or two before we give it precisely. To describe an experiment or trial with random outcomes, we need three things.
    1. A sample space \(\Omega\text{,}\) the set of all possible outcomes.
    2. A family \(\mathcal{F}\subseteq 2^{\Omega}\) of permissible events. (It is less obvious that we need this.) By \(2^\Omega\) we mean the powerset of \(\Omega\text{.}\)
    3. An assignment \(P\) of probabilities (numbers) to events.

    Definition 1.1.3.

    A probability measure \(P\) on \((\Omega,\mathcal{F})\text{,}\) where \(\Omega\) is a set and \(\mathcal{F}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field of subsets of \(\Omega\text{,}\) is a function \(P\colon \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]\) satisfying the following properties.
    1. \(\displaystyle P(\Omega) = 1\)
    2. \(P\) is countably additive, meaning that for any countably infinite list \(A_1,A_2,\dots\) of pairwise disjoint (\(i\ne j\) implies \(A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset\)) members of \(\mathcal{F}\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} P\left( \bigcup_{n \ge 1} A_i \right) = \sum_{n\ge 1} P(A_n). \end{equation*}
    The two conditions in Definition¬†1.1.3 together with the fact that \(P(A) \ge 0\) for every \(A \in \mathcal{F}\) are often called the three Axioms of Probability.

    Definition 1.1.4.

    A collection \(\mathcal{F}\) of subsets of \(\Omega\) is a \(\sigma\)-field if it satisfies the following three conditions.
    1. \(\displaystyle \emptyset\in\mathcal{F}\)
    2. If \(A_1,A_2,\dots\in\mathcal{F}\) then \(\bigcup_{n\ge 1} A_n \in \mathcal{F}\) (closure under countable unions).
    3. If \(A\in\mathcal{F}\) then \(A^c \in \mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
